# MC/DC at the WebAssembly Level: DWARF-Grounded Reconstruction and a Coverage-Lifting Argument

*witness v0.2 technical paper. Draft, 2026-04-24.*
*Companion to `DESIGN.md` §"v0.2 — MC/DC at Wasm level" and `docs/research/mcdc-bytecode-research.md`.*

---

## Abstract

Modified Condition/Decision Coverage (MC/DC) is the structural-coverage criterion DO-178C requires for Level A avionics software. It is defined on source-level decisions, but every shipped MC/DC tool — Clang's source-based MC/DC, rustc's `-Zcoverage-options=mcdc`, Ferrous and DLR's in-progress Rust effort — instruments at an intermediate representation while still in possession of the source AST. None of these tools survives a third-party WebAssembly module produced by an unknown front end and stripped of its IR-time coverage decoration. witness v0.2 instruments at the post-rustc Wasm level. It reconstructs source-level decisions from `br_if` and `br_table` sequences using DWARF-in-Wasm metadata, falls back to v0.1's strict per-instruction counting when DWARF is absent, supports decisions of any condition count (no LLVM 6-cap), and ships a soundness argument — coverage lifting — that projects Wasm-level MC/DC evidence back to source-level decisions under a stated assumption about DWARF correctness. This paper formalises the reconstruction algorithm, states and discusses the lifting theorem, places witness against rustc-mcdc, Clang, JaCoCo, wasmcov, Whamm, and Wasabi, and documents which regulatory bars v0.2 clears and which it does not.

---

## 1. Motivation

DO-178C Annex A Table A-7 objective 5 requires MC/DC for Level A software. The same standard has accepted, since DO-178B in 1992, that MC/DC for C may be measured *post-preprocessor* — on the translation unit after `#include` expansion and macro substitution have run, not on the developer's pre-preprocessor source. Nobody argues this is illegitimate. Assessors take it as given. The reasons are practical: the preprocessor erases information (a macro that hides a decision behind a single token) and inserts information (a `__LINE__` that places code in a header), and the only stable artefact a tool can instrument is the post-preprocessor output. The C standard treats the preprocessor as a separate translation phase precisely because it is.

Wasm produced by `rustc` is the structural analog. Rust's `&&`, `||`, pattern `match`, conditional `if let` chains and `?` propagation all lower through HIR, MIR, LLVM IR, and finally the Wasm ISA. By the time a `.wasm` file lands on disk, short-circuit evaluation has become a sequence of `br_if` instructions, pattern matches have become `br_table` jumps, and `cfg`-gated arms have evaporated. The sequence is monotone information loss with respect to the source AST and monotone information gain with respect to what the runtime will actually execute. Measuring coverage on Wasm is the structural analog of measuring it post-preprocessor, with one advantage: the Wasm instruction set has a machine-readable specification[^wasm-spec] and a reference test suite[^wasm-tests] that the C language standard does not.

JaCoCo[^jacoco-counters] is the lateral. It does instruction, line, and branch coverage on JVM class files. It does not do MC/DC[^jacoco-no-mcdc] and never has. The JVM ecosystem has accepted bytecode-level coverage as a shipped, useful, and assessor-readable artefact since JaCoCo's first releases. The "no MC/DC" gap has not stopped its adoption. JaCoCo proves the *bytecode* part of "MC/DC on bytecode" is precedented.

The remaining question is whether the *MC/DC* part is recoverable from Wasm at all, given that short-circuit lowering shreds the source-level decision boundary. v0.2 says yes, conditional on DWARF being present, and ships the algorithm and the soundness argument that justify that yes.

A reasonable objection: the Safety-Critical Rust Consortium and the Rust Project have a 2026 Project Goal[^rust-project-goal] for Rust-level MC/DC, with Ferrous Systems and DLR among the contributors. Once that effort lands, the Wasm-level approach looks redundant. It is not. The two measurement points have different blind spots. rustc-mcdc instruments before LLVM optimisation passes run; it cannot detect a decision that was preserved in MIR but reshaped at the LLVM IR or Wasm level. witness measures on the artefact the runtime will actually execute; it loses some source structure but catches optimisation-stage divergence. The overdo principle from the parent verification-chain post[^overdo] applies: layer independent techniques, do not pick one and tune it. Both ship. Both compose. This paper assumes both will exist and explains witness's non-overlapping contribution.

## 2. Background

### 2.1 MC/DC: the formal definition

The standard reference is Chilenski and Miller (1994)[^chilenski]. The four-clause unique-cause MC/DC definition:

1. Every entry and exit point of the program is invoked.
2. Every decision in the program takes every possible outcome.
3. Every condition in a decision takes every possible outcome.
4. Each condition in a decision is shown to independently affect the decision's outcome.

Clause 4 is the load-bearing one. Two test cases that differ only in the value of condition `c_i` and produce different decision outcomes demonstrate that `c_i` independently affects the decision. Vilkomir and Bowen[^vilkomir] later gave the operational semantics version, distinguishing unique-cause MC/DC, masking MC/DC, and unique-cause-plus-masking MC/DC; DO-178C accepts any of the three forms.

The definition is stated on a *decision* — a Boolean expression composed of conditions — and on its *conditions* — atomic Boolean predicates not further decomposed. At source level the boundary is syntactic: in `if a && (b || c)`, the decision is the whole expression and the conditions are `a`, `b`, `c`. Below source level the boundary is not syntactic. It has to be reconstructed.

### 2.2 Wasm structured control flow

Wasm has structured control flow. The relevant instructions:

- `if … else … end` — conditional with two arms.
- `br_if L` — pop `i32`, branch to label `L` if non-zero.
- `br L` — unconditional branch to `L`.
- `br_table L_0 L_1 … L_n L_default` — pop `i32` selector, branch to `L_selector` (or `L_default` if out of range).
- `block`, `loop` — labelled regions.

The key property for coverage: there are no arbitrary jumps. Every branch lands on a label that lexically dominates the branch site. This rules out the irreducible CFGs that complicate JVM coverage analysis and lets witness reason about decision boundaries with structural recursion on the AST that walrus[^walrus] already provides.

### 2.3 DWARF-in-Wasm

Wasm's custom-section mechanism carries DWARF debug information identical in form to DWARF on native binaries[^dwarf-in-wasm]. Each instruction can be mapped (by offset within the function) to a `(file, line, column, decision_marker?)` tuple. `rustc` emits DWARF for Wasm targets when the user requests debug info; it is preserved through `wasm-opt` if the optimiser is asked to keep it. The decision-marker DWARF extension required for fine-grained MC/DC reconstruction does not yet exist in upstream rustc; v0.2's reconstruction algorithm degrades to line-and-column granularity when only standard DWARF is present, and v0.2's roadmap section §11 describes the rustc-side change that would close the gap.

### 2.4 The SCRC 2026 Project Goal context

The Safety-Critical Rust Consortium[^scrc] and the Rust Project agreed in 2026 on a Project Goal for MC/DC support in Rust. The motivation, stated in the Rust blog post announcing the goal[^rust-blog-2026-01], is the DO-178C Level A requirement and the absence of any qualified Rust-level MC/DC tooling. Ferrous Systems and DLR are among the named contributors. The expected output is an extension of `-Zcoverage-options=mcdc`, today an unstable rustc feature backed by the LLVM coverage encoder.

The published academic anchor for that work is "Towards Modified Condition/Decision Coverage of Rust"[^arxiv-2409]. The paper instruments at the rustc HIR-to-MIR boundary, threads condition identity through MIR coverage intrinsics, and emits LLVM-compatible bitmap counters. Their measurement point is upstream of LLVM optimisation. witness's measurement point is downstream of LLVM optimisation and of any Wasm-level tooling (`wasm-opt`, `wasm-snip`, post-link rewriters). The two are non-overlapping by design. This paper engages with their contribution in §8.

## 3. Problem statement

Short-circuit lowering at the Wasm level destroys the source-level decision boundary. A worked example:

```rust
fn check(a: bool, b: bool, c: bool) -> bool {
    a && b && c
}
```

After rustc + LLVM lowering and Wasm emission, a representative compiled body (annotated):

```wat
(func $check (param $a i32) (param $b i32) (param $c i32) (result i32)
  block (result i32)
    local.get $a
    i32.eqz
    br_if 0       ;; branch 1: short-circuit on !a
    local.get $b
    i32.eqz
    br_if 0       ;; branch 2: short-circuit on !b
    local.get $c
    i32.eqz
    br_if 0       ;; branch 3: short-circuit on !c
    i32.const 1
    return
  end
  i32.const 0)
```

Three `br_if` instructions, each with its own offset. The original Rust expression was a single decision with three conditions. v0.1 of witness counts each `br_if` independently — strict per-instruction counting. That is correct branch coverage, but it is not source-level MC/DC. To satisfy clause 4 of MC/DC at the *source* level, we have to assert that test cases independently exercise `a`, `b`, and `c` *as conditions of one decision*, not as three unrelated branches.

The dichotomy:

- **Strict interpretation.** Each `br_if` is its own one-condition decision. MC/DC clause 4 is trivially satisfied: a one-condition decision has one condition, and that condition independently affects the outcome by definition. v0.1 ships this. It is sound — the resulting evidence is not *false* — but it claims less than the source-level standard requires of the equivalent expression.
- **Reconstructed interpretation.** Group the three `br_if` instructions back into one decision with three conditions. Demand the test suite exercise the four MC/DC tuples (`abc=011, 101, 110, 111` for `a && b && c` is one minimal set) and verify that the corresponding Wasm-level branch counters reflect that exercise. v0.2 ships this when DWARF is present.

The hard problem is defining "decision granularity at the Wasm level" *operationally*. Operational means: the algorithm must run on a `.wasm` file with DWARF, must produce a deterministic grouping, and must be re-checkable by a third party from the same inputs. The reconstruction-algorithm formalism in §5 is the answer. The soundness claim that the reconstruction maps to a source-level decision is §7.

The strict-vs-reconstructed dichotomy is not a binary. v0.2 produces *both* views in the same report. Strict is the fallback (no DWARF, ambiguous DWARF, or pathological CFG fragmentation); reconstructed is the headline. The honest assessment in §10 is explicit about which decisions land in which bucket on real codebases.

## 4. Background — terminology fixed for the rest of the paper

A small glossary used uniformly below.

- **Branch site** — a single Wasm instruction at a specific function-and-offset that may transfer control (`br_if`, `if`, the entry of an `if-else` arm, a `br_table`).
- **Branch counter** — an exported mutable `i32` global named `__witness_counter_<id>` whose value is the number of times the branch site at `id` was taken. v0.1 invariant: one counter per branch site.
- **`br_if` chain** — a maximal sequence of `br_if` instructions in CFG order whose targets all reach the same join point and whose source DWARF lines are all within a contiguous lexical region. Defined formally in §5.
- **Decision** — a Boolean expression that the developer wrote, at source level, as a single short-circuit chain or conditional. The reconstruction algorithm aims to recover this set.
- **Condition** — an atomic Boolean predicate within a decision. After lowering, a condition typically corresponds to one `br_if` in the chain.
- **Decision marker** — a DWARF attribute (or, absent that, a `(file, line, column)` synthetic key) that uniquely identifies the source decision a Wasm instruction came from.
- **Strict fallback** — the v0.1 interpretation: each branch site is its own one-condition decision. Always sound; gives less credit than reconstruction.

## 5. The reconstruction algorithm

### 5.1 Inputs and outputs

The algorithm takes:

- `M` — a Wasm module post-instrumentation, with one branch site per `br_if`, per `if-else` arm, and per `br_table` target.
- `D` — DWARF-in-Wasm metadata for `M` from the `.debug_info`, `.debug_line`, and (if present) `.debug_decisions` custom sections.
- `manifest` — witness's side-channel JSON listing every branch site with its `(function_index, instr_index, kind)`.

It produces:

- `groups` — a partition of branch sites into equivalence classes, each class being one source-level decision with N conditions.
- `singletons` — branch sites that did not land in any group; treated under the strict-fallback rule.

### 5.2 Algorithm in pseudocode

```
algorithm RECONSTRUCT(M, D, manifest) -> (groups, singletons):
    by_function = manifest.group_by_function()
    groups = []
    singletons = []
    for fn in M.functions:
        sites = by_function[fn.index].sorted_by_instr_index()
        i = 0
        while i < len(sites):
            site = sites[i]
            if site.kind not in {br_if, if_else_arm}:
                singletons.append(site)
                i += 1
                continue

            chain = [site]
            j = i + 1
            seed_decision = decision_marker(site, D)
            while j < len(sites):
                next = sites[j]
                if next.kind != br_if:
                    break
                if not on_same_join_path(chain[-1], next, fn.cfg):
                    break
                if decision_marker(next, D) != seed_decision:
                    break
                if line_of(next, D) not in lexical_extent(seed_decision, D):
                    break
                chain.append(next)
                j += 1

            if len(chain) > 1 and seed_decision is not None:
                groups.append(make_decision(chain, seed_decision))
                i = j
            else:
                singletons.append(site)
                i += 1

    return (groups, singletons)
```

The grouping rule has four predicates that all must hold:

1. **Lexical decision marker matches.** Either both instructions carry the same `.debug_decisions` decision id, or both fall under the same `(file, line, column)` synthetic key when only standard DWARF is available.
2. **Same source line set.** Every instruction in the chain maps under DWARF to the lexical extent of the seeded decision (one source line for the common case; multiple lines when the developer wrote the chain across line breaks).
3. **CFG connectivity.** Each `br_if` in the chain branches either to the same join point as the previous `br_if` or to a unique next-condition site that flows into the same join. This rules out unrelated `br_if` instructions that happen to share a line because of a macro.
4. **Kind compatibility.** Only `br_if` and the entry instructions of `if-else` arms participate. `br_table` is handled separately (§6); arbitrary `br L` cannot start or extend a chain.

`make_decision(chain, marker)` produces a decision record with:

- A list of conditions, one per `br_if` in `chain`, each with its own counter id and its own DWARF source location.
- The decision-level outcome, computed from the joined CFG (true = "fell through past every short-circuit"; false = "took at least one short-circuit branch out of the chain").
- The MC/DC table: enumeration of the 2^N condition combinations, with rows marked covered when the corresponding counter pattern was observed across the test run.

### 5.3 Worked example

Take the `a && b && c` example from §3. Three `br_if` instructions at function offsets 4, 9, 14. Their DWARF mappings (under standard DWARF):

| offset | line | column |
|---|---|---|
| 4 | 7 | 8 |
| 9 | 7 | 13 |
| 14 | 7 | 18 |

Same line, different columns. With only standard DWARF the synthetic marker is `(file, line=7)` — column intentionally dropped because each condition lives at a different column within one decision. All three `br_if` instructions carry the same line-7 marker. CFG connectivity: each falls through to the next on the success arm and branches to a single shared exit on the failure arm. Kind compatibility: all `br_if`. The chain groups; the algorithm emits one decision with three conditions.

If the developer had written:

```rust
if a && b { foo(); }
if c { bar(); }
```

then offsets 4 and 9 would share a line (assume both on line 7) but offset 14 would land on line 8. Step 2 of the predicate (same source line set) cuts the chain after offset 9. Algorithm emits one decision with two conditions for `a && b`, plus a singleton for `c` that is treated as a one-condition decision under strict fallback.

### 5.4 Edge cases

**Macro expansion.** `assert_eq!(left == right || tolerance > 0)` expands to a body with multiple `br_if` instructions, all carrying the same `__FILE__`, `__LINE__`, `__COLUMN__` of the macro invocation. Standard DWARF would collapse them to one synthetic key and group them as one decision. That is wrong: the macro body and the user-written argument are different decisions. A `.debug_decisions` extension that distinguishes macro-internal conditions from macro-argument conditions would solve this; absent that, the algorithm emits a `decision_kind: "ambiguous_macro"` flag in the report and treats each contributing `br_if` as a singleton. Honest fallback over false credit.

**Inlining.** When function `g` is inlined into `f`, the inlined `br_if` instructions carry `g`'s source line, not `f`'s. The chain-extension predicate uses the *inlined* line, so a decision in `g` continues to group correctly even after inlining. The DWARF inline-call-site information is used to disambiguate two inlined copies of the same `g` decision in different parts of `f`.

**CFG fragmentation by optimisation.** LLVM optimisation passes — particularly `simplifycfg` and `early-cse` — may split a single source decision across multiple basic blocks that the optimiser later joins via a `phi` node. The Wasm lowering serialises this back into structured control flow, but the resulting `br_if` sequence may be interleaved with non-`br_if` instructions (a `local.set` between two conditions, for example). The algorithm's "kind compatibility" predicate skips intervening pure operations under a configurable allowlist (`local.set`, `local.tee`, `i32.const`); breaks the chain on side-effecting instructions or on `call`. The allowlist is the only configuration knob in the algorithm. v0.2 documents it in the manifest header so reports are reproducible.

**Constant folding.** rustc's optimiser may fold `if a && true && c` to `if a && c`, removing one `br_if`. The witness report is silent on conditions that no longer exist in the Wasm. This matches LLVM's behaviour: removed conditions are not reported as uncovered. The honest read: the strict-vs-source-level coverage gap is exactly the constant-folded condition count, which is structurally bounded by the source AST.

**Reordered conditions.** The optimiser may reorder `a && b` to evaluate `b && a` if the order is provably side-effect-free at the type level. The reconstruction does not detect this. The reported decision still has two conditions; the MC/DC table still enumerates four combinations; the test suite either covers the four or it does not. The semantic meaning of "condition 1 independently affects the outcome" is preserved under reordering of side-effect-free expressions, so the lifting argument in §7 is unaffected.

### 5.5 Determinism

The algorithm is deterministic for a given `(M, D, manifest)`. It iterates branch sites in instruction-offset order; uses sorted DWARF lookups; produces an output partition in offset order; and writes the report in canonical-JSON form. This is not "morally deterministic" — it is bitwise-stable across re-runs and across machines, because witness's report-emit phase already enforces stable JSON ordering for v0.1 (`AGENTS.md` invariant, REQ-003).

## 6. Per-target `br_table` counting

### 6.1 The case for per-target counting

Rust pattern `match` lowers to `br_table` when the matched type is a small integer or when the compiler decides the table is more efficient than nested compare-and-branch. A 5-arm `match` becomes a `br_table` with five `br_table` targets plus a default. v0.1 instruments `br_table` with a single counter incremented every time the instruction executes — useful for "did we ever hit this match?" but mute on which arm. Per-target counting is the next granularity. The same DWARF that maps `br_if` chains to source decisions also maps `br_table` targets to source-level match arms, so the implementation work and the decomposition work flow from one upstream change.

### 6.2 Helper-function-call instrumentation

Two implementations were considered (DEC-008):

**Inline `i32.eq` chain.** Before the `br_table`, emit one `if (selector == k) { incr counter[id][k] }` per target. For an N-target table this is O(N) instructions per match site, plus a counter global per target. The instruction count blows up for wide tables; default 8-bit selectors reach 256 targets in extreme cases.

**Helper function call.** Emit a synthetic helper:

```wat
(func $__witness_brtable_<id> (param $sel i32) (result i32)
  ;; bounds-check selector; clamp to N-1 on out-of-range so default arm counts cleanly
  local.get $sel
  i32.const <N>
  i32.lt_u
  if (result i32)
    local.get $sel
  else
    i32.const <N>   ;; index of the synthetic "default" counter
  end
  ;; load the appropriate counter, increment, store back
  local.tee $idx
  global.get $__witness_brtable_base_<id>
  i32.add
  local.tee $addr
  i32.load
  i32.const 1
  i32.add
  local.get $addr
  i32.store
  local.get $sel)   ;; return the selector unchanged for the following br_table
```

Followed by:

```wat
local.get $sel
call $__witness_brtable_<id>
br_table L_0 L_1 … L_n L_default
```

Constant cost per `br_table` execution: one call, one load, one store, plus the bounds check. Independent of N. This is what v0.2 ships.

The counters for `__witness_brtable_<id>` live in a separate exported memory region rather than in N+1 separate `__witness_counter_*` globals; this keeps the runtime export iteration cheap and avoids hitting wasmtime's practical limit on the number of exported globals (currently around 10,000) when modules have many wide tables.

### 6.3 Cost analysis vs. inline `i32.eq` chain

For an N-target table executed K times:

| approach | static instructions | dynamic cost per execution | counter exports |
|---|---|---|---|
| inline chain | ~5N | O(N) compares + 1 increment | N+1 globals |
| helper call | ~30 (helper) + 3 (call site) | 1 call + 1 load + 1 store | 1 memory region |

Helper-call wins on every axis except micro-benchmarks for N ≤ 3, where the inline chain's branch-prediction friendliness slightly edges the call. v0.2 chooses the helper unconditionally for simplicity and report-format uniformity. Future work could switch on N if profiling shows the small-table case matters.

### 6.4 DWARF mapping to match arms

DWARF's `.debug_info` stores match-arm source extents under a `DW_TAG_lexical_block` per arm when rustc is invoked with `-Cdebuginfo=2`. The reconstruction step pairs each `br_table` target index with the corresponding `DW_TAG_lexical_block` and exposes a `match_arm_label` field in the manifest:

```json
{
  "id": 47,
  "kind": "br_table_target",
  "function_index": 12,
  "instr_index": 88,
  "target_index": 2,
  "match_arm_label": "match_arm@src/main.rs:42:9-44:6",
  "decision_id": 19
}
```

The `decision_id` ties the target back to a single match-decision record so the report can summarise "match decision at line 42 covered 4 of 5 arms".

## 7. Coverage-lifting

### 7.1 What "lifting" means

The reconstruction algorithm produces, for a Wasm module M with DWARF D, a set of decisions `D_w` at the Wasm level. Each `D_w` has a witness-and-checker construction (DEC-010): the DWARF metadata claims `D_w` corresponds to a specific source-level decision `D_s`, and the reconstruction algorithm honours that claim when it groups branch sites. The lifting question: does MC/DC on `D_w` imply MC/DC on `D_s`?

### 7.2 The lifting theorem

**Theorem (lifting).** Let M be a Wasm module produced by `rustc` (with LLVM at any optimisation level) from a Rust source program S. Let D be the DWARF-in-Wasm metadata for M. Let `D_w` be a Wasm-level decision produced by the reconstruction algorithm of §5 from `(M, D)`. Let `D_s` be the source-level decision in S that D claims `D_w` corresponds to. Under assumption A (below), if MC/DC clauses 1–4 are satisfied for `D_w` by a test run, then MC/DC clauses 1–4 are satisfied for `D_s` by the same test run.

**Assumption A.** The DWARF claim is correct: the rustc + LLVM lowering preserves the *independence-of-condition relation* of `D_s` — that is, for each condition `c_i` in `D_s`, the corresponding `br_if` instruction (or `br_table` target) in M is reached on at least the executions of M where `c_i` is the condition that toggles the outcome of `D_s`, and the lowering does not introduce a spurious dependency on any other source-level condition.

The theorem itself is straightforward once A is granted. MC/DC clause 4 demands that for each condition `c_i`, two test cases exist that differ only in `c_i`'s value and produce different decision outcomes. If A holds, those two test cases at the source level produce two test cases at the Wasm level with the same property on `D_w`. The reconstruction's per-condition counters reflect those executions. Conversely, if the test suite covers `D_w`'s condition `c_i^w` in the unique-cause sense, A's preservation of independence means `c_i^s` is also covered in the unique-cause sense at the source level.

The work is in defending A.

### 7.3 Translation validation as the bridge

Pnueli, Siegel, and Singerman (1998) introduced translation validation[^pnueli] as the technique of proving, *after* compilation, that the compiled program preserves the semantics of the source. CompCert is the flagship example for C. The argument shape is: for each (source, compiled) pair, generate a verification condition and discharge it; the compiler need not be verified, but the translation result is.

Coverage lifting is a structurally similar but distinct claim. The conventional translation-validation question is "do these two programs have the same input/output behaviour?" The coverage-lifting question is "does this property of the compiled program (a set of branch counters reaching certain values) entail the corresponding property of the source program?" The first is a semantic-equivalence question on programs; the second is a property-preservation question on observation-equipped programs.

No prior work in the surfaced literature formalises coverage lifting. The closest neighbours:

- The CompCert project[^compcert] proves end-to-end semantic preservation for a C compiler, but coverage is not a tracked property.
- Translation validation for individual LLVM passes[^translation-validation-llvm] proves equivalence of pre- and post-pass IR for specific transformations.
- The Alive2[^alive2] tool checks LLVM optimisation passes against a refinement relation, focused on undefined behaviour rather than coverage.

The witness contribution under this lens: name coverage lifting as a property-preservation question, state the assumption explicitly, and ship the reconstruction algorithm as the empirically-defensible witness-and-checker construction (DEC-010) without claiming to have proved A.

### 7.4 Defending assumption A: two paths

**Direct proof.** Demonstrate that `rustc -O n` for some specific `n` preserves the independence-of-condition relation. This requires either a verified rustc front-end (out of reach), a verified LLVM (out of reach), or per-pass translation-validation evidence layered up the chain (in reach for some passes, not for `simplifycfg`-class transformations that do not preserve a clean condition-by-condition correspondence). Direct proof is years of work even for a restricted optimisation profile. v0.2 does not attempt it.

**Witness-and-checker.** Trust the DWARF emitted by rustc; build a small, qualified checker that verifies the DWARF assertion against the Wasm structure. The checker does not need to prove the *correctness* of the DWARF — only its *consistency* with the reconstruction algorithm's output. If the DWARF claims a `br_if` chain corresponds to source decision `D_s` with three conditions, the checker verifies that the chain has three `br_if` instructions, each with the expected DWARF marker, and that the chain's CFG topology matches the short-circuit semantics of `D_s`. A discrepancy fails the check; a match passes the check.

The witness-and-checker variant is what v0.2 ships. The qualified checker itself is v1.0 (REQ-011), under the Check-It pattern[^check-it] that the parent verification-chain post commits the whole stack to. The DO-330 burden under this construction is to qualify the checker — small, deterministic, testable — not the emitter, which is rustc + LLVM + walrus combined and has no realistic qualification path.

The honest read on the state of the art: under the witness-and-checker construction with v0.2's reconstruction, *MC/DC at the Wasm level is meaningful evidence for source-level MC/DC, conditional on DWARF correctness, with the conditional made explicit in every report*. No invisible step. That is the bar the regulatory framing in §9 leans on.

### 7.5 What the lifting does *not* claim

It does not claim that source-level MC/DC implies Wasm-level MC/DC. The other direction — projecting source-level coverage *down* to Wasm — is a wholly different question and witness does not address it. It does not claim that all source-level decisions are recoverable from any `.wasm`. Constant folding, dead-code elimination, and inlining can erase source-level decisions outright; the reconstruction reports only what survives. It does not claim that two semantically-equivalent Rust programs that differ only at source level (e.g., `a && b` versus `b && a` for side-effect-free `a`, `b`) produce identical witness reports — they may differ in condition order within a decision, and the report exposes that difference. Honest mechanical observation, not source-AST recovery.

### 8.1 Direct engagement with Garcia-Lopez et al., "Towards MC/DC of Rust"

Garcia-Lopez and colleagues (arXiv:2409.08708; AIAA JAIS final form[^arxiv-2409-aiaa]) describe a Rust MC/DC implementation that instruments at HIR-to-MIR lowering by extending `BranchInfoBuilder` with an `MCDCState`. Their measurement point is upstream of LLVM. Their encoder is LLVM's bitmap. Their condition cap is six, inherited from the bitmap encoding. Their primary contribution, as I read it, is the algorithm by which `MCDCState` threads decision and condition identity through the HIR-to-MIR lowering: the `&&` and `||` operators are recognised at HIR level and decomposed into MIR coverage intrinsics that LLVM's coverage backend later folds into a bitmap counter.

Three points of engagement:

**Their work and witness do not overlap.** Their measurement point is HIR-to-MIR; ours is post-Wasm-emission. A test that demonstrably covers a six-condition decision under their tool may, after `wasm-opt -O3` reorders, partially folds, or splits the lowered code, no longer evidently cover the same decision at the Wasm level. witness catches that gap. Conversely, witness cannot recover decisions that LLVM constant-folded out of existence; their tool catches that. Neither subsumes the other. This is precisely the overdo case from §1.

**The 6-cap is not a witness constraint.** The arxiv paper is explicit that the cap is an LLVM-encoder property. witness uses one exported `i32` global per branch site; there is no encoder, no bitmap, no cap. Decisions of any condition count work. This is the most concrete differentiation under the SCRC Project Goal: when a real codebase has a 7+ condition decision (rare but real — long property-validation chains, multi-key dispatch tables), witness measures it; rustc-mcdc declines.

**Their assumption set and ours are different.** They assume rustc's HIR-to-MIR lowering preserves source-level decision identity, which is checkable by inspection of `BranchInfoBuilder` (open-source). They do not need to assume anything about LLVM. witness's assumption A in §7 is stronger — it covers the whole pipeline through Wasm emission — but it is *checkable* because DWARF carries the assertion explicitly and the v1.0 Check-It checker can verify the assertion's structural consistency with the Wasm. In a stack that ships both, the union of assumptions is the union of trust: trust rustc-mcdc up to MIR; trust the witness-and-checker construction up to Wasm; let translation validation (loom, v0.4) bridge the rest.

This is a complementary citation, not a competitive one. The arxiv paper makes the case for measuring MC/DC on Rust at all; witness makes the case for measuring it once more, on the artefact that actually ships, and using the lift to project the result back. Both are cited in the regulatory framing of §9 as evidence the chain layer has multiple shipped or shipping techniques rather than one.

## 9. Regulatory framing

### 9.1 DO-178C / DO-330

DO-178C Table A-7 objective 5 ("Test Coverage of Software Structure") lists MC/DC as the structural coverage required for Level A. The standard does not specify the *measurement point*. DO-178B and its successors have accepted measurement on the post-preprocessor C translation unit since 1992.

I have not located the standard text in open-access form. The widely-used secondary references[^do-178-secondary] all describe post-preprocessor C MC/DC as the accepted norm. The Rapita Systems guidance for DO-178C[^rapita-mcdc] explicitly states that post-preprocessor measurement is the ordinary practice for C codebases. The FAA's RTCA-coordinated Order 8110.49[^faa-8110-49] references DO-178C MC/DC obligations without specifying a pre/post-preprocessor preference. **TODO: source the DO-178C clause that explicitly accepts post-preprocessor C measurement, ideally from RTCA-DO-248C errata commentary that is sometimes circulated at conferences.** Until that primary source is filed, this paper's regulatory argument relies on the widely-acknowledged secondary characterisation that post-preprocessor C measurement is the de facto standard.

The structural argument for accepting post-rustc Wasm MC/DC under the same precedent:

1. The C preprocessor is a translation phase in the C standard. rustc + LLVM is a translation phase in the Rust pipeline. Wasm emission is a translation phase in the Rust + Wasm pipeline.
2. DO-178C accepts measurement after the preprocessor's translation. By structural analogy, it should accept measurement after rustc's translation, provided the analog holds: the post-translation artefact is a stable, well-defined object with a machine-readable specification.
3. Wasm has both: the [Wasm core specification](https://webassembly.github.io/spec/core/) is a publicly maintained machine-readable document; the [Wasm test suite](https://github.com/WebAssembly/spec/tree/main/test) is a reference test corpus. The C standard's preprocessor specification is, by comparison, more diffuse.
4. The witness-and-checker construction (DEC-010, §7) addresses the residual concern that post-translation measurement loses source-level decision identity: DWARF carries the assertion; the v1.0 checker (Check-It, REQ-011) verifies it; the qualification burden falls on the small checker, not the entire toolchain.

DO-330 ("Software Tool Qualification Considerations") supplies the qualification framework. Under the witness-and-checker construction, the *emitter* (witness itself) is a TQL-5 development tool: a bug in witness may produce wrong coverage data, but the data is checked downstream and a discrepancy is detectable. The *checker* (v1.0) is a TQL-1 verification tool: it must be qualified because its output is what the assessor reads. The Check-It pattern[^check-it] collapses the qualification problem to the small checker, exactly per the parent post's commitment.

### 9.2 The C-macro precedent in detail

The post-preprocessor C precedent is worth unpacking because it is the rhetorical anchor.

In a typical C codebase, MC/DC is measured on a translation unit of the form `gcc -E foo.c > foo.i; gcov-mcdc foo.i`. The macros have been expanded; the `#include`d headers have been inlined; `#ifdef` blocks have been stripped. The result is a single `.i` file with line directives that allow the coverage tool to map each instrumented condition back to the developer-visible source.

What is *lost*: the source-level macro boundary. A condition that the developer wrote inside a macro now appears at the macro's call site under macro expansion. Two macro invocations of the same macro produce two structurally-similar conditions in the `.i` file with different line directives. The MC/DC tool counts them as separate conditions, which is technically correct: each invocation site is a distinct decision. The developer's intuition that "the macro is one thing" is not what gets measured.

What is *kept*: every condition the source actually instantiated. No condition is invented; no condition is dropped; the measurement is mechanical on the post-preprocessor text. The qualification of the C preprocessor is well-understood and decades old; a coverage tool inspecting `.i` files is in well-trodden territory.

The witness analog: every Rust decision the source actually instantiated, after `cfg`-pruning, monomorphisation, and LLVM optimisation, is present in the Wasm module and accessible to witness's reconstruction. The "macro boundary" analog is the source-decision boundary, and the §5 algorithm with DWARF support reconstructs it. What is lost: source-level decisions that LLVM constant-folded to nothing (analog: macros that expand to nothing in C). What is kept: every decision that survived the lowering, with a DWARF-carried assertion of which source decision it came from.

Both kinds of measurement — post-preprocessor C and post-rustc Wasm — are mechanical, deterministic, and re-checkable. The qualification of the toolchain (preprocessor, rustc + LLVM) is treated as out of scope for the coverage tool; the coverage tool's own qualification is bounded by the coverage tool's own complexity.

### 9.3 ISO 26262, IEC 61508, EN 50128 — adjacent applicability

**ISO 26262 Part 6 Table 9** lists MC/DC as "highly recommended" for ASIL D and "recommended" for ASIL C. The standard does not specify the measurement point. Tool Confidence Level qualification under ISO 26262-8 §11 is the gap; under the witness-and-checker construction, the checker is a TCL 3 tool and the emitter is a TCL 1 development tool. Same shape as DO-178C/DO-330.

**IEC 61508-3 Table A.5** lists MC/DC as "highly recommended" for SIL 3 and SIL 4. The standard accepts §7.4.4 "proven-in-use" arguments as a qualification route for tools that lack a dedicated qualification campaign. The JaCoCo precedent (bytecode-level branch coverage, accepted in regulated contexts) supports a §7.4.4 argument for the witness emitter under sufficient deployment volume. v0.2 does not yet have that volume; v0.3 (rivet integration) and v1.0 (Check-It) are when the argument becomes serviceable.

**EN 50128 Table A.17** for railway software lists MC/DC as "highly recommended" for SIL 3 and SIL 4. EN 50128's tool-qualification framework (Table A.18) is structurally similar to DO-330's TQL system; the same checker-not-emitter argument applies.

The adjacency point: any of these three standards, like DO-178C, accepts MC/DC as the structural coverage criterion without specifying the measurement point. The C-macro post-preprocessor precedent, while specifically a DO-178C/B history, generalises by analogy: structural coverage on the post-translation artefact is the conventional place to measure when source-level direct measurement is impractical.

### 9.4 The overdo principle

The parent verification-chain post[^overdo] argues that regulated software should layer independent verification techniques rather than pick one and tune it. The relevant quote, from the post:

> "Adding a second, independent tool at the same layer is often cheaper than tightening the first one beyond diminishing returns."

In the chain layer where MC/DC lives, witness and rustc-mcdc are two independent tools. They do not share an implementation; they do not share an assumption set; they do not share a measurement point. Their coverage signals are additive evidence, not redundant. The cost of running both on a CI build is two coverage runs, not two qualification campaigns — qualification is per-tool but evidence is union-able. The overdo position: ship both; let the assessor pick which evidence they want; do not bet a certification campaign on a single technique having no blind spots.

This positioning is the core regulatory argument for v0.2: not "witness is the MC/DC story for Rust" but "witness is one MC/DC measurement point on a chain that should have multiple". The blog post's worked example for this principle is Airbus's adoption of Astrée alongside other techniques for DO-178C DAL A. The witness analog is rustc-mcdc plus witness: same chain layer, different blind spots, both run, evidence merged in rivet (REQ-007).

### 9.5 What the regulatory framing does *not* claim

It does not claim that v0.2 is qualified under DO-330. v0.2 is a development-stage tool; qualification is a v1.0 milestone (REQ-011). It does not claim that the post-preprocessor-C precedent is automatically transferable to Wasm; the analogy is structural, the standards committees have not opined directly, and the open question of where exactly the analogy bends is genuine. It does not claim that the witness-and-checker construction closes assumption A; A remains open and the checker is the path to closing it. It does not claim that running witness alone clears DO-178C Table A-7 objective 5; only the union of witness + a qualified Rust-level MC/DC tool + sound translation validation across the Rust → Wasm bridge plausibly clears the objective, and v0.2 is one piece of that union.

## 10. Honest assessment

Per the overdo-blog C7 constraint — name what does *not* clear the bar.

### 10.1 What v0.2 clears

- ✅ MC/DC for Rust decisions of any size when DWARF is present, via the §5 reconstruction algorithm.
- ✅ Strict per-`br_if` / per-`if-else` fallback when DWARF is absent or ambiguous (REQ-005, DEC-006).
- ✅ Per-target `br_table` counting via the helper-function-call pattern (REQ-013, DEC-008).
- ✅ Subprocess `--harness <cmd>` mode with file-based handshake for non-wasmtime runtimes (REQ-014, DEC-009).
- ✅ Coverage-lifting writeup with the soundness *theorem* and assumption A stated explicitly (this paper, REQ-016, DEC-010).
- ✅ Determinism: same `(M, D, manifest, run-data)` produces bit-identical reports across re-runs and machines.
- ✅ Differentiation from rustc-mcdc on condition count (no 6-cap), measurement point (post-Wasm vs. pre-LLVM), and modules-without-source applicability.

### 10.2 What v0.2 partially clears

- ◐ Soundness *proof* of lifting. v0.2 ships the witness-and-checker construction with assumption A explicit. The full proof would require either a verified compilation chain or a chain of translation-validation evidence layered up the optimisation stack. Direct proof is years of work; v0.2 ships the practical variant.
- ◐ DWARF decision-marker quality. Standard DWARF emitted by rustc today does not include a `.debug_decisions` extension that distinguishes macro-internal conditions from macro-argument conditions. v0.2's algorithm degrades to `(file, line, column)` synthetic markers and falls back to strict treatment when the synthetic marker is ambiguous. Closing this gap is rustc-side work tracked in §11.
- ◐ Regulatory framing. The post-preprocessor C precedent is widely acknowledged but the verbatim DO-178C clause has not been sourced from open-access material in this paper (TODO above). The structural analogy is strong but not yet primary-sourced.
- ◐ Component-model coverage. v0.2 handles core Wasm modules. Components are extracted to core modules first when possible; full component-aware instrumentation is v0.4 (REQ-012). The walrus backend's component support is the bottleneck (DEC-011).

### 10.3 What v0.2 does not clear

- ❌ Component-model coverage with composition awareness (v0.4).
- ❌ rivet evidence-format integration (v0.3, REQ-007).
- ❌ sigil in-toto predicate emission (v0.3, REQ-008).
- ❌ DO-330 / TCL / EN 50128 tool-qualification artefact (v1.0, REQ-011).
- ❌ Translation-validation evidence consumption from loom (v0.4, REQ-010).
- ❌ Direct soundness proof of assumption A (research; possibly v1.0+ depending on rustc / loom verified-pass progress).

### 10.4 Risks to v0.2 specifically

**Pathological CFG fragmentation.** LLVM optimisation may split a source decision across non-contiguous Wasm regions in ways that break the chain-extension predicate's CFG-connectivity requirement. The algorithm degrades to strict-fallback cleanly, but the resulting coverage report claims less than the source-level decision could have claimed. Mitigation: pre-flight `wasm-opt -O0` or `-O1` runs to reduce fragmentation; the fragmentation rate is a metric the v0.2 report exposes for diagnostic purposes.

**DWARF stripping.** Production Wasm modules sometimes ship without DWARF. The reconstruction algorithm cannot run; the report falls back to strict per-`br_if`. This is a known limitation; v0.2 documents it in the report header so consumers see when reconstruction did not engage.

**rustc DWARF-emission bugs.** rustc has bugs in DWARF emission for Wasm targets that surface intermittently[^rustc-dwarf-bugs]. The witness-and-checker construction depends on DWARF correctness for assumption A; a bug here is a regression in the lifting argument's reliability. Mitigation: the v1.0 Check-It checker is structurally able to detect inconsistencies between DWARF and Wasm structure and flag them as report errors rather than silently producing wrong coverage.

**Macro-heavy codebases.** Codebases with deep macro stacking (`thiserror`, `serde_derive`, custom DSL macros) produce DWARF where macro-internal and macro-argument decisions collapse to the same line number. The algorithm's `decision_kind: "ambiguous_macro"` flag is the honest fallback, but the resulting coverage credit is reduced from what the source actually exercised. Closing this gap is the rustc-side `.debug_decisions` work.

## 11. Future work

### 11.1 v0.3 — rivet and sigil integration

v0.3 lands the integration with the surrounding PulseEngine ecosystem.

- **rivet evidence format (REQ-007).** Coverage reports become first-class rivet evidence linked to requirement IDs. A `(REQ-NNN, decision_id, mcdc_outcome)` triple is what rivet ingests, with the witness JSON report as the underlying artefact. This closes the "traceability" chain layer that the parent verification-chain post explicitly assigns to rivet rather than to witness.
- **sigil in-toto predicate (REQ-008).** Coverage reports become signable in-toto predicates that travel in sigil attestation bundles alongside per-transformation predicates from loom and meld. The predicate format follows the in-toto SLSA conventions; the witness output is a single canonical-JSON document that signs cleanly.

### 11.2 v0.4 — translation validation and the component model

- **Post-loom translation validation (REQ-010).** loom produces Z3-backed translation-validation evidence for Wasm-to-Wasm transformations (e.g., post-`wasm-opt`). When loom certifies that pre- and post-transformation modules are equivalent, witness coverage measurements on the post-transformation module project to coverage claims on the pre-transformation module under loom's equivalence relation. This is the "translation validation bridges the chain" move from the parent post applied directly to coverage.
- **Component-model coverage (REQ-012).** Wasm components wrap core modules and add interface-level adaptation. v0.4 either extracts core modules, instruments each, and re-composes; or migrates the rewrite backend from walrus to wasm-tools (DEC-011) for direct component support. The choice depends on walrus's component-model maturation by mid-2026.
- **Rust `.debug_decisions` extension.** A rustc-side change to emit a decision-marker DWARF extension that distinguishes macro-internal conditions from macro-argument conditions, removing v0.2's `decision_kind: "ambiguous_macro"` fallback. This is upstream rustc work; the v0.2 paper's macro-expansion edge case (§5.4) motivates it.

### 11.3 v1.0 — Check-It qualification

The Check-It pattern[^check-it] from the parent verification-chain post is the qualification strategy.

- **Qualified checker (REQ-011).** A small, deterministic checker that consumes `(M, D, manifest, run-data, witness-report)` and verifies the witness report's claims against the inputs. Verifies: every reported decision corresponds to a `br_if` chain or `br_table` matching the algorithm's predicates; every counter value is consistent with the Wasm structure; every MC/DC table entry is supported by the run-data.
- **DO-330 dossier scoped to the checker.** The checker is small enough to qualify under DO-330 TQL-1; the emitter (witness itself) is a TQL-5 development tool whose output the qualified checker validates. This collapses the DO-330 burden from "qualify the entire toolchain" (infeasible) to "qualify a small checker" (tractable), exactly per the parent post's commitment.

The Check-It pattern also closes assumption A in the right way: not by *proving* A (which would require verified compilation), but by reducing the trust footprint of A from "the entire rustc + LLVM + walrus pipeline" to "the DWARF emitter and the small checker", which is a much smaller and more tractable trust target.

### 11.4 Research extensions

Two threads are worth flagging even though they are not on the current roadmap.

- **Coverage lifting as a formal property-preservation claim.** §7's lifting theorem is stated semi-formally. A fully formal version, perhaps in a proof assistant, would name the property-preservation relation explicitly and ground it in translation-validation theory[^pnueli][^translation-validation-llvm]. This is a paper, not a code milestone.
- **Empirical study of the strict-vs-reconstructed gap.** On real codebases, what fraction of `br_if` instructions land in reconstructed groups versus singleton fallback? What fraction of decisions hit the macro-ambiguity case? The gap is a function of codebase style (heavy `?` propagation increases short chains; heavy `match` increases `br_table` use; macro-heavy code increases ambiguity). v0.2 instruments the gap as a report-level metric; a longitudinal study across a representative corpus is the deliverable.
